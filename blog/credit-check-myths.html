Does a Mortgage Credit Check Hurt Your Score? Busting the Myths

Book a Free Consultation

Pick a time that works for you — no obligation

One of the most common things I hear from first-time buyers is some version of this: "I don't want you to pull my credit because I heard that ruins your score." I completely understand why people think this — but it's one of the most persistent myths in the mortgage world, and it can actually hurt you if you believe it and avoid getting pre-approved. Let me set the record straight on mortgage credit checks in Canada and what they actually do (and don't do) to your score.

Hard Inquiries vs. Soft Inquiries: What's the Difference?

First, let's talk about the two types of credit checks. A soft inquiry happens when you check your own credit, or when a lender does a background check without a formal application — like when you get pre-approved credit card offers in the mail. Soft inquiries don't affect your credit score at all. You can check your own credit as often as you want with no impact.

A hard inquiry happens when a lender formally reviews your credit as part of a credit application — like applying for a mortgage, a car loan, or a credit card. Hard inquiries can affect your score, but here's the thing: the impact is much smaller than most people think, and it fades quickly.

How Much Does a Mortgage Credit Check Actually Hurt Your Score?

A single hard inquiry for a mortgage application typically drops your credit score by about 5 to 10 points. That's it. If your score is 720, it might temporarily dip to 710 or 715. Within a few months — and certainly within a year — your score recovers as the inquiry ages.

Here's some important context: a 5-10 point drop is unlikely to change which mortgage tier you fall into. The difference between a 715 and a 720 score usually doesn't change your mortgage rate or your approval at all. The differences that matter are usually larger jumps — like going from 650 to 700, or from 680 to 720 — which separate different risk tiers in the lender's eyes.

Rate Shopping Won't Destroy Your Score

Here's something most people don't know: Canada's credit bureaus (Equifax and TransUnion) use a process called "inquiry clustering" or "rate shopping" protection. If multiple mortgage inquiries show up within a short period — typically 14 to 45 days — they're counted as a single inquiry for scoring purposes.

This makes complete sense when you think about it. If you're shopping for the best mortgage rate, you shouldn't be penalized for doing your homework. The credit scoring models recognize that a consumer who gets their mortgage pulled by five different lenders over two weeks is likely just rate shopping, not desperately applying for credit everywhere.

So if I submit your file to three or four lenders to get you the best rate, you don't get hit with three or four hard inquiries. You effectively get hit with one.

The Real Danger: Avoiding Credit Checks Altogether

Here's where the myth actually causes harm. I've had clients who were so worried about their credit score that they put off getting pre-approved for months. They thought they were protecting themselves, when in reality they were:

Shopping without knowing their real budget. Without a pre-approval, you don't know how much you can actually afford. I've seen people fall in love with homes above their qualification range, write offers, and then face crushing disappointment when the financing falls apart.

Missing issues on their credit report. When I pull a credit report, we often find errors — wrong addresses, a collection account that's not theirs, a missed payment that was actually paid. Catching these early gives you time to dispute and fix them before they cost you an approval. Avoiding the credit check means these problems sit undetected.

Losing the house they wanted. In a competitive market, a pre-approval letter gives you credibility as a buyer. Sellers and realtors take pre-approved buyers more seriously. Without it, you're at a disadvantage.

What Actually Hurts Your Credit Score?

Since we're busting myths, let me tell you what really does damage your score — because these are the things worth worrying about:

Missed or late payments. Payment history is the single biggest factor in your credit score, accounting for about 35% of the calculation. One missed payment — especially on a mortgage or car loan — can drop your score significantly. Pay everything on time, every time.

High credit utilization. If you're using 80% or 90% of your available credit, your score suffers. Try to keep utilization below 30% across all your credit products. Paying down credit card balances is one of the fastest ways to boost your score.

Closing old credit accounts. Counterintuitively, closing a credit card you've had for 10 years can hurt your score by reducing your average account age and your total available credit. If you want to simplify your wallet, don't close your oldest card — just cut it up and stop using it.

Applying for multiple new credit products in a short time. While mortgage rate shopping gets clustered, applying for two new credit cards, a car loan, and a line of credit all in the same month sends a different signal — one of financial stress. Avoid opening new credit in the months leading up to your mortgage application.

When Should You Pull Your Credit?

My advice: pull your credit at least three to six months before you plan to apply for a mortgage. This gives you time to see where you stand, identify any errors or issues, and take steps to improve your score if needed. You can check your own credit for free through Equifax or TransUnion — that's a soft inquiry, and it won't affect your score at all.

Then, when you're ready to move forward, let me pull a formal credit check as part of your pre-approval. We'll know exactly what we're working with, and we can start shopping lenders with confidence.

The Bottom Line on Mortgage Credit Checks

A mortgage credit check won't ruin your score. It won't disqualify you from a great rate. And it definitely won't follow you around forever. The temporary, small dip in your score is worth the clarity, protection, and credibility that comes with a proper pre-approval.
